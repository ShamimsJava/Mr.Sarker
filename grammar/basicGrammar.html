6.1 Comparative and Superlative Adjectives 1

Comparative adjectives compare two things. Superlative adjectives compare more than two things.

Comparative and Superlative Adjectives - Examples

Comparative adjectives compare two things. Adjectives that contain only one syllable or end in "-y" use "-er" to form comparatives. For adjectives ending in "-y", change the "y" to "i" before adding the "-er".

Superlative adjectives compare more than two things. Adjectives that contain only one syllable or end in "-y" use "-est" to form superlatives. For adjectives ending in "-y", change the "y" to "i" before adding "-est".

Comparative and Superlative Adjectives - How to use
The word "than" is used after comparatives.
  1. Lisa is taller than Jane.
  2. Joe is smaller than Dan.
  3. California is typically warmer than Alaska.
  4. His costume is scarier than mine.
  5. John is happier than David.
  6. Julie is prettier than Karen.
Superlatives are typically accompanied by the word "the".
  1. Daniel is the tallest boy on the basketball team.
  2. Lucy is the smallest girl in her class.
  3. Thursday was the warmest day of the week.
  4. That was the scariest movie I have ever seen.
  5. Sally is the happiest girl in the world.
  6. I think roses are the prettiest flower.

6.4 Basic Regular Adverbs

Adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. An adverb tells more about a verb in the sentence. An adverb describes more about an adjective in the sentence. An adverb modifies another adverb in the sentence.

Adverbs - Modifying Verbs

An adverb tells more about a verb in the sentence.
  1. The fire engine runs fast.
  2. Listen to his speech carefully.
  3. I browse the web frequently.

Adverbs - Modifying Adjectives

An adverb describes more about an adjective in the sentence.
  1. The news is very surprising!
  2. The coffee is extremely hot, so be careful.
  3. Nature is really amazing!

Adverbs - Modifying Other Adverbs

An adverb modifies another adverb in the sentence.
  1. The meteorologist is almost always right in predicting the weather.
  2. My computer is running very fast after updating the system.
  3. She works extremely well with younger children.

6.5 Adverb - Either

"Either" is used in negative sentences. It is used to indicate agreement. "Either" usually comes at the end of the sentence or clause.

"Either" as an adverb
  1. My brother does not speak Spanish. I don't speak Spanish either.
  2. I don't like milk. I don't like cheese either.
  3. My friends cannot attend the wedding. I cannot attend the wedding either.
  4. I don't eat meat. My husband does not eat meat either.
  5. My mother does not like carrots. I don't like carrots either.
  6. My dad does not want a dog. My mom does not want one either.
  7. He won't be late. I won't be late either.
  8. My roommate does not have a computer. I don't have a computer either.

6.6 Adverb - So

"So" has two different uses. "So" can be an adverb or a coordinating conjunction. "So" can be used the same way as "too". They are both used to give an adjective or adverb a more extreme meaning. However, "so" is used to describe the word as stronger in intensity. "Too" is used to describe the word as being more than necessary or enough.

"So" as an adverb

"So" before an adjective or adverb gives the word a more extreme meaning. "So" is used to describe the word as stronger in intensity.
  1. The food was so spicy.
  2. He was so angry with her.
  3. I am so happy to hear good news.
  4. She was moving so quickly. I couldn't keep up.
  5. He is doing so well since his promotion.
  6. Our dog is getting so old.
  7. He drives so fast.
  8. You are so mean.

6.7 Comparative and Superlative Adverbs 1

Comparative adverbs compare two things. Superlative adjectives compare more than two things.

Comparative and Superlative Adverbs - Examples

Comparative adverbs compare two things. Adverbs that contain only one syllable or do not end in "-ly" (in adverb form) use "-er" to form comparatives.

Superlative adverbs compare more than two things. Adverbs that contain only one syllable or do not end in "-ly" (in adverb form) use "-est" to form superlatives. Just like superlative adjectives, adverbs ending in "y", change the "y" to "i" before adding "-est" to form superlative adverbs.

Comparative Adverbs - How to use

The word "than" is often used after comparatives.
  1. He has a faster car than you.
  2. The squirrel moves quicker than the raccoon.
  3. Sally works hard, but Linda works harder.
  4. The hiker climbed high, but his partner climbed higher.

Superlative Adverbs - How to use

The word "the" is often used before superlatives.
  1. Daniel jumped the highest.
  2. Lucy sang the loudest in the choir.
  3. Louis ran the fastest.
  4. Sam arrived the earliest.

6.10 Compound Nouns with Some, Any, and No

Compound nouns are nouns made up of two or more words. The words that make up a compound noun are words that can stand alone. Compound nouns with "some", "any", "no" are most often used with "thing", "body", "one", and "where".

Some

A compound noun with "some" indicates that a person, place, or thing is not known or stated. It is often used with "thing", "body", "one", and "where". It is generally used in positive sentences and questions.
  1. There is something under the bed.
  2. Somebody is knocking on the door.
  3. Someone is waiting to see you.
  4. I put my keys somewhere and now I can't find them.
  5. Do you want to eat something?
  6. Is somebody at the door?
  7. Is someone at the door?
Any

A compound noun with "any" indicates that a person, place, or thing is not known or stated. It is often used with "thing", "body", "one", and "where". It is generally used in negative sentences and questions.
  1. He doesn't know anything about the incident.
  2. He doesn't know anybody at the party.
  3. He doesn't know anyone at the party.
  4. He didn't go anywhere all weekend.
  5. Did anyone turn in the wallet?
  6. Is there anything to eat?
  7. Did you go anywhere this weekend?
No

A compound noun with "no" indicates that there is no person, place, or thing. It is often used with "thing", "body", "one", and "where".
  1. They did nothing about the problem.
  2. Nobody cared about what happened.
  3. They are going nowhere.
  4. No one cares about me.
